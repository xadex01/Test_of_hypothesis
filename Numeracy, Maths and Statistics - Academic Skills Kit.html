Hypothesis Testing with the Poisson Distribution

Introduction

Testing hypotheses with the Poisson distribution is very similar to testing them with the binomial distribution. If the probability is greater than αα, the level of significance, then the null hypothesis is accepted. If it is less than αα, we accepted the alternative hypothesis.

Worked Example

Worked Example

An existing make of car is known to break down on average one and a half times per year. A new model is introduced and the manufacturer claims that this model is less likely to break down. Ten randomly selected cars break down a total of eight times within the first year. Test the manufacturer's claim at a 55% significance level.

Solution

Let XX be the number of break downs of the new model of car in a year. Since we have an average rate and the data is discrete, we need to use a Poisson distribution. So X∼Poisson(λ)X∼Poisson(λ) with λ=1.5λ=1.5. The null and alternative hypotheses will be

H0:λ=1.5,H1:λ<1.5.H0:λ=1.5,H1:λ<1.5.

We need to decide whether P[X≤8]<αP[X≤8]<α, where α=0.05α=0.05 is the significance level. Firstly, the expected number of breakdowns λt=1.5×10=15λt=1.5×10=15. We use the cumulative tables with λt=15λt=15 and x=8x=8 to see P[X≤8]=0.0374P[X≤8]=0.0374. P[X≤8]=0.0374<0.05=αP[X≤8]=0.0374<0.05=α so we accept the alternative hypothesis. The average rate of breakdowns has decreased.
